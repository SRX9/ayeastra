*Part of the [AyeAstra PRD](README.md). Where we enter the market, who buys, how we sell, and in what order we expand.*

## The market sandwich (strategic reality)

AyeAstra launches into a squeeze:

- **From below — AI assistants.** ChatGPT Pulse delivers proactive daily briefings; scheduled Deep Research, Gemini, and Perplexity produce sourced "what did my competitors do this week" summaries for $20–200/month. Any feature that amounts to "AI summarizes public web changes" is commoditized on arrival.
- **From above — AI-enabled incumbents.** Klue, Crayon, Contify, and AlphaSense (which acquired Tegus) all have AI layers, established customers, and are consolidating.

**The escape is to ship, from day one, the three things neither side can replicate easily:**

1. **Persistent evidence/diff archive** — timestamped before/after snapshots of every change, per entity, forever. ChatGPT has no memory of what a competitor's pricing page looked like in March. This archive compounds into something irreplaceable.
2. **Customer-specific business grounding** — alerts and briefings mapped to the customer's strategy, competitors, target segments, positioning, priorities, and decision cadence captured during onboarding.
3. **Workflow embedding** — the briefing in the Monday leadership cadence, alerts in Slack/email, battlecards where the team already discusses competitive moves.

These are not Phase-3 aspirations. They are launch requirements, because they are the durable differentiation.

## Launch wedge: Competitive Watch

**Evidence-backed competitive intelligence for B2B SaaS teams.**

> *"We watch your competitors and tell you what changed, why it matters to your strategy, and what to do this week — with evidence."*

### Why this wedge

People pay when a product ties to (a) money won or lost, (b) a deadline, or (c) personal accountability. A generic executive briefing fails all three — it's interesting, nobody is accountable for it, and it's the first cut in a budget review. A weekly competitive decision briefing is stronger: it tells PMM, founders, product, and sales enablement what changed, which narrative or plan is affected, and what action should happen now.

The weekly executive briefing is the **forwardable visibility artifact** that gets AyeAstra in front of the CEO and drives expansion. The **paid core** is decision support: pricing, positioning, launches, roadmap implications, campaign angles, enablement updates, and competitive narrative.

### Initial ICP

- B2B SaaS, Series A–D, 50–500 employees, 3–20 meaningful competitors.
- Competitive pressure directly affects positioning, launches, campaigns, sales narrative, and board conversations.
- Has a PMM, founder's office, strategy, product, or sales enablement owner who is already doing manual competitive tracking.
- **Launch vertical (decided): martech / sales-tech / RevOps B2B SaaS** — companies selling marketing, sales, and revenue software. Why: the most competitively intense SaaS category (constant pricing/feature churn = weekly signal volume), competitors with rich public footprints (pricing pages, changelogs, launch posts — all Tier-1 sources), buyers who live on LinkedIn where the distribution flywheel runs, and perfect dogfooding — AyeAstra's own competitors sit in this category, so public demo briefings double as our own market intelligence. Widen only after the first 20 customers.

Avoid at launch: large enterprises (6–12 month procurement), companies needing regulatory/security-grade accuracy, very small startups with no budget, "all industries" positioning.

### Buyer and champion

- **Budget owner:** Founder/CEO (smaller companies); Head of Product Marketing, Strategy, or CI (larger).
- **Champion path:** PMM / founder's office → product marketing leadership → CEO/CRO/Product. One buying center first, compounding trust later.
- **Daily users:** PMM, founders' office, sales enablement, sales leadership, product managers.

The first sale requires exactly one budget owner. Cross-functional value comes later.

### The killer workflow

> Every Monday morning, the leadership, product, marketing, and sales teams get a sourced briefing: what changed in the market and competitors, why it matters to the company's current priorities, and what to do this week.

Contents: top competitor moves · pricing/packaging changes with before/after diffs · launches and changelog updates · messaging/positioning shifts · impact by target segment and strategic priority · updated battlecards · recommended actions with owners · source link + confidence on every material claim.

## Go-to-market motion

1. **Founder-led, demo-led sales.** Build the demo on the prospect's real competitors; send a sample briefing *before* the call; show three things they didn't know; convert on proof — time saved, move caught, decision changed. Self-serve signup means a convinced prospect can subscribe on the spot.
2. **Distribution flywheel (the primary channel).** Cold outbound in 2026 lands in inboxes drowning in AI SDR spam. Instead, let the engine market itself:
   - **Public category briefings** — "What changed in [category] this week" pages and LinkedIn teardowns, generated by our own pipeline. Proof-of-product, SEO, and lead-gen in one motion; compounds weekly.
   - Communities and partnerships with PMM/GTM consultants as secondary channels.

## Expansion path

Three rules, in priority order:

**Rule 1 — Depth before breadth.** Improve source coverage, evidence quality, scoring, battlecards, and briefing usefulness before adding new departments or complex internal-system access. The first product should feel excellent without requiring customer-system access.

**Rule 2 — Expand within the competitive workflow.** Module order: **Competitive Watch → Product & Market Watch → Brand & Narrative Watch → Account Watch.** Same buyer family, inherited trust, shared entity graph.

**Rule 3 — Regulatory and Security Watch are long-term vision, not operating plan.** Different buyers, liability-grade accuracy bars a startup cannot clear, and sales cycles that would starve us. They stay in the architecture story and the investor narrative. Revisit only with strong inbound demand and the resources to meet a specialist bar.

### The module replacement bar (hard gate)

A module is listed on the pricing page only when it clears all of:

- **Coverage** — monitors the sources the buyer already checks manually.
- **Precision** — high-priority findings are usually useful, not noisy.
- **Evidence** — every material claim links to source, timestamp, and changed content.
- **Workflow fit** — delivers into the channel where that buyer already works.
- **Artifacts** — produces things the team uses immediately (battlecards, briefs, memos).
- **Replacement** — covers 60–80% of the buyer's manual workflow or lightweight point-tool usage.

One mediocre module poisons trust in all the others. The catalog grows **one module at a time**, each clearing this bar. The modular pricing architecture ([Part 4](04-business.md)) is designed up front; the catalog fills in over years.

## Moats, in the order we build them

1. **Evidence/diff archive** — months of timestamped per-entity history nobody else has (starts day 1).
2. **Customer-specific context graph** — onboarding interview + feedback + priority history; switching means losing the grounding (starts day 1).
3. **Workflow embedding** — the Monday cadence, Slack/email routing, battlecards in the competitive workflow (starts at first customer).
4. **Outcome-tuned scoring** — feedback and action data make relevance customer-specific (Phase 2).
5. **Cross-module entity graph** — "Stripe" is one object across competitive, product, market, and brand intelligence (Phase 2–3).
6. **Pattern library** — validated predictive patterns per industry and entity (Phase 3+).

The moat is never "we have AI." Model access is rented by everyone; memory, context, and workflow are earned.
